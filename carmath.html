CarMath

Compare the true cost of different vehicle plans over time

How does this calculator work?

The problem

Deciding whether to keep your car, repair it, or buy something new is tricky. The sticker price is only part of the story — gas, insurance, maintenance, loan interest, and depreciation all add up over time. It's hard to compare options when costs happen at different times.

What this calculator does

CarMath converts all future costs into today's dollars using a concept called present value. The idea is simple: $1,000 five years from now is worth less than $1,000 today, because you could invest that money and earn a return in the meantime. This calculator applies that logic to every cost — gas, maintenance, insurance, loan payments, and resale value — so you can compare options on a level playing field.

How to use it

Set your assumptions at the top (gas price, how far you drive, and how many years you want to plan for). Then fill in the details for each option. You can chain multiple cars together if you expect to switch partway through, or add a gap period if you plan to go without a car for a while. The ranking at the bottom shows which plan costs the least in today's dollars.

Tips

Don't worry about getting every number perfect — reasonable estimates are fine. Try changing the inputs to see how sensitive the result is. If a small change flips the ranking, the options are close enough that other factors (reliability, comfort, lifestyle) should drive your decision.

All recurring costs are converted to present value using the annuity formula: PV = Payment × (1 − (1+r)−n) / r

Costs for later cars are discounted to today based on when that car period begins.

Total = ∑ per car: Down Payment + Upfront + PV(Loan) + PV(Gas) + PV(Maintenance) + PV(Insurance) − PV(Residual Value)
