Glossary

Money factor

The lease equivalent of an interest rate, written as a small decimal like .00125. Multiply by 2400 to get the APR. Dealers mark this up — always ask for the "buy rate."

Residual value

What the bank estimates the car will be worth at lease end, as a % of MSRP. Set by the bank, non-negotiable. Higher residual = lower depreciation = lower payment.

Capitalized cost (cap cost)

The lease version of "purchase price" — the price financed through the lease, including fees rolled in.

Cap cost reduction

Money down on a lease. Risky: if the car is totaled early, you lose it. Most experts recommend $0 down.

Acquisition fee

Bank fee to originate the lease ($595–$995 typically). Non-negotiable but should not be charged twice.

Disposition fee

Charged at lease end if you return the car ($350–$500). Often waived if you lease/buy another car from the same brand.

Doc fee

Dealer processing fee. State-capped in many states. Non-negotiable at some dealers, but above $500 is suspect.

MSRP vs invoice

MSRP = sticker price. Invoice = what the dealer paid the manufacturer (approximately — holdback complicates this). Negotiate down from MSRP, not up from invoice.

APR vs interest rate

APR includes certain fees and is the true cost of borrowing over a year. Interest rate is just the rate on the principal. APR is what you compare.

Gap insurance

Pays the difference between what you owe and what the car is worth if it's totaled. Built into most leases. On a loan, credit unions sell it for 1/3 of the dealer price.

ADM / market adjustment

Dealer markup above MSRP. In normal markets, decline it. Walk away if they won't remove it.

Dealer holdback

A rebate the manufacturer pays the dealer (2–3% of MSRP) after the sale. Means they can sell below invoice and still profit.

Buy rate vs sell rate

Buy rate is what the bank offers the dealer. Sell rate is what the dealer offers you. The difference is dealer profit. Ask for the buy rate.

Trade-in equity

Value of your trade minus what you still owe. Negative = "upside down / underwater." Never roll negative equity into a new loan.

1% rule (lease heuristic)

A "good" lease has a monthly payment ≤ 1% of MSRP (pre-tax). $40k car → aim for under $400/mo.

Before you sit down

  • Get pre-approved. Walk in with a credit union pre-approval. It sets a ceiling on the APR the dealer can offer.
  • Know the OTD you'll accept. Decide your walk-away number before you walk in.
  • Separate the three conversations. Price of car, trade-in value, and financing are independent. Dealers love to mix them — don't let them.
  • Ignore the monthly payment. Negotiate OTD. Monthly is a knob the dealer twists to hide the real numbers.

Lease red flags

  • Marked-up money factor. Ask "is this the buy rate?" — they must tell you. Mark-ups of .0004 = ~1% APR in disguise.
  • $0 down, really? Any "down payment" on a lease is a cap cost reduction and is lost if the car is totaled early.
  • Disposition fee surprise. Ask what it is up front. Often $350–$500 at return.
  • Mileage tier. 10k/yr is common default — going to 12k or 15k is cheap up front, way cheaper than $0.25/mile overage.
  • Acquisition fee charged twice. Look for a "bank fee" and an "acquisition fee" on the same sheet.
  • Cap cost inflated quietly. Check that selling price on the lease matches what you negotiated.

Finance red flags

  • Rate markup. If your pre-approval is 5.5% and they offer 6.9%, that extra 1.4% is dealer reserve. Negotiate or walk.
  • Term extension trick. "We lowered your payment!" often means they extended 60 → 72 months and raised total interest by thousands.
  • Negative equity rollover. If your trade is worth less than you owe, rolling it into the new loan compounds the problem. Pay it off separately if you can.
  • Payment packing. Salesperson quotes a monthly that silently includes extended warranty and GAP. Demand the itemized OTD.

Add-ons to decline (or cut hard)

  • VIN etching. $200–$500 for something that costs $20 at an auto shop. Decline.
  • Nitrogen tires. Decline. Air is 78% nitrogen already.
  • Paint + fabric protection. Decline. Wax and Scotchgard do the same thing.
  • Dealer alarm / wheel locks. Decline unless already installed and they remove the charge.
  • Extended warranty. Negotiable by thousands. Never buy at first quote. Can be purchased later.
  • GAP insurance. Worth buying — but from a credit union for ~1/3 of dealer price.
  • Doc fee above state cap. Google "[your state] doc fee cap" — if theirs is higher, push back.

Power phrases

  • "What is the out-the-door price?" Forces them off monthly-payment games.
  • "Is that the buy rate or the sell rate?" Signals you know what's up.
  • "I need to see an itemized breakdown." Surfaces padded fees.
  • "Remove that line and I'll sign today." Specific, actionable, hard to refuse.
  • "I'll think about it." Then leave. The best offers often come by text that evening.
